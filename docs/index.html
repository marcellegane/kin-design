Our History | Our Future

KIN is a new initiative aiming to bring black activists and organisers together from across the UK to collaborate, strategise and support each other.

The first convening took place from Friday 17th to Sunday 19th August 2018 where around thirty activists and organisers came together to explore the history of national and international social movements, to share organising practice and to contextualise what achieving collective liberation looks like within the UK.

Quobna Ottobah Cugoano was an #African abolitionist, author, philosopher and one of the first Black published activists against slavery in England. Born in 1757 in Ajumako, in what is now #Ghana, he was kidnapped at age 13 while playing near his house, and sold as a slave. He was transported to #Grenada. In his book he described the horrific working conditions in sugar plantations on the island. In 1772, he is “bought” and transported to England by Alexander Campbell. There he was baptised, manumited (“freed”) and given an English name, John Stuart, in Saint James’s church, #Picadilly. From 1784, he worked for the official painter of the royal court, Richard Cosway. With a group of other African abolitionists and intellectuals, he started the Sons of Africa. They wrote newspaper articles, petitioned the governement and wrote letters to other abolitionists. In 1787, Cugoano published a radical attack on slavery entitled Thoughts and Sentiments on the Evil and Wicked Traffic of the Slavery and Commerce of the Human Species. The book was sent to the royal family, who stayed firmly opposed to abolition of slavery. It went through at least 3 printings in 1787, was translated into French, and Cugoano went around Britain to promote it. It was considered very radical at the time: in it Cugoano encouraged slaves to rebel and run away to become free (when most people, even in abolitionist circles, wouldn’t approve of a rebellious slave and would expect them to have a more passive and “resilient” behaviour while waiting to be saved); also, being a devout Christian, Cugoano’s arguments rely heavily on Scriptures and threaten all enslavers to eternal damnation. It is considered a philosophical and theological text, where the author’s main intent is to prove the immoral nature of slavery. Unfortunately, nothing is known of his life after that point. The only image we have of him is in this drawing by Richard Cosway, where he serves grapes to Richard and his wife. He should be remembered as one the 1st African activists in England (with an interesting perspective, as he was also a British citizen) and co-founder of one of the first African activist groups in England.

On Sunday January 18, 1981, a fire killed 13 young Black people, aged 15 to 21, at 439 #NewCross Road, southeast #London. They were celebrating the joint 16th birthdays of Angela Jackson and Yvonne Ruddock. Victims of the fire: Andrew Gooding, Owen Thompson, Patricia Johnson, Patrick Cummings, Steve Collins, Lloyd Hall, Humphrey Geoffrey Brown, Roseline Henry, Peter Campbell, Gerry Paul Francis, Glenton Powell, Paul Ruddock, Yvonne Ruddock - and Anthony Berbeck, survivor of the fire, who was traumatised and committed suicide 2 yrs later. Many contradictions appeared in the police investigation: they first defended the theory that the fire was the result of an attack with a fire bomb. But then the police started defending the idea of a fight between boys at the party that accidentally started the fire. Several of these men later claimed, such as Robert McKenzie in a 2001 Guardian article, that they felt like the police had their version of the events they were trying to enforce, and that they pressured the young people in telling them “what they wanted to hear”. Local community worker Ros Howells remembers the general tone of the press as criminalising and victim-blaming, implying that something illegal happened at the party. Protests took place in cities across Britain in response to the police’s behaviour in this investigation. The New Cross Massacre Action Committee was set up, and organised on March 2nd the 'Black People's Day of Action'. More than 20 000 people marched for 8 hours, from New Cross to Hyde Park. At Blackfriars Bridge, police decided to cut the march in two, which was seen as an attempt to weaken or maybe even to stop the march. The inquest into the deaths of the 13 teenagers saw criticism of the police. The victims' families challenged the procedure and while the High Court agreed that the summing-up by the coroner was inaccurate, it did not overturn the verdict. A 2nd inquest was held in 2005, which also resulted in an open verdict. In March '17, George Ruddock, a survivor of the fire, was reported in an article by @thevoicenewspaper to be gathering signatures to put pressure on Parliament for a public inquiry. #BlackBritishHistory
